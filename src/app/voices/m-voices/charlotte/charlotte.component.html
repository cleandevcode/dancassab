VOICES

CHARLOTTE
KIDGER

Charlotte Kidger is a London based UK Maker. Kidger graduated from Leeds College of Art and Central Saint Martins and has a background in Colour and Material Design. Her practice is led with a hands-on approach to investigating how industrial waste materials can be reprocessed and transformed into sculptural and functional objects. As an experimental material-led designer her work is driven by the urge and curiosity to transform often unwanted and valueless materials into desirable and functional objects.

‘ I have been influenced by local waste materials I have sourced here that maybe I wouldn’t have otherwise come across. ’ — CHARLOTTE KIDGER.

DC: What are your earliest memories of creative output?

CK: When I was around 12 my nan taught me how to knit. She used to be a textile designer and would knit a lot of my clothes for me. I think her creativity was passed down to me as well as from my Dad who was also very good and sketching and painting.

DC: Have you always been interested in sculpture as an art form?

CK: I became more aware and into sculpture during my Masters at Central Saint Martins. Prior to this I was a printed textile designer / surface designer so worked more with soft materials. It was during my MA I transitioned to harder materials and then looked at sculpture as a way of communicating the materials into 3D forms.

DC: Has growing up in the UK greatly affected work? How did it shape your aesthetic?

CK: I have been influenced by local waste materials I have sourced here that maybe I wouldn’t have otherwise come across. My aesthetic has subconsciously been shaped by marrying the languages of industrial and classical architecture not necessarily associated with the UK. I wouldn’t say I have been influenced by anything that iconically British though.

DC: Has living in London had an impact your post-grad work?

CK: I would say I have been given great opportunities by galleries and design festivals that take place here. The exposure and support have impacted my work through giving me a platform to continue developing my practice.

DC: Working in this medium, does location significantly impact or influence your resources and materials?

CK: Very much so given the foundation of my practice is set up around a waste material I discovered here. My work is all created from a signature blend of materials that have been locally sourced from factories in the UK. I have turned this resource into a secondary raw material through finding a second life for it.

‘ The colours and forms in my work are continually influenced by one another. I first started using circular forms and corrugated textures after making prototypes from corrugated sheets of plastic and forms found in building merchants - from here I then developed them into my own language. ’ — CHARLOTTE KIDGER.

DC: Which materials do you use most often in your signature work?

CK: There is one main material I use within all my work. Polyurethane foam dust, which is a type of plastic created as a by-product from CNC industries and is predominant in all my work. Through combining it with a hand-dyed resin I have created a durable, alluring a highly versatile new material.

DC: From where do you draw the majority of influences on color and form?

CK: The colours and forms in my work are continually influenced by one another. I first started using circular forms and corrugated textures after making prototypes from corrugated sheets of plastic and forms found in building merchants - from here I then developed them into my own language. It was important that simplicity was celebrated and the forms were a way of celebrating the materials and highlighting their characteristics. I tend to use colours that have been influenced by nature, particularly the blues and greens with hints of Chartreuse from lichen seen on rocks.

DC: How do you conceptualize your pieces? What does your typical process and lead time look like? How does this differ when approaching commissioned work for retail and hospitality?

CK: My process consists of creating 3D forms from layers of wood or plastic sheeting, mould making with Silicone and Jesmonite and then cold-casting with my signature blend of materials. The materials are cast in layers of different colours, they are then left to set overnight. The most exciting part of the process is de-moulding the next day - there is always some unexpected developments, but these are often the best part of the pieces. A typical lead time for a bespoke piece is 8-12 weeks - this is from initial sketching to completion. My in-house range tends to have a 4 week lead time as sometimes we have to source the materials and this can vary on the waste the factories are producing.

DC: What is the ultimate goal of your art?

CK: I would love for my work to be part of permanent collection in a museum. A museum would be the ultimate goal. To have it recognised as telling a part of history would be amazing. It is also important that my work continues to sit between art and design, blurring boundaries of what craft can mean through my material choices and being part of a bigger debate around society’s industrial waste streams and mass production.

DC: Would you consider your work sustainable? What are some of the greatest misconceptions of sustainability in design?

CK: Yes - I think my work sits within a larger conversation around sustainability. Through working with waste dust and finding a way to give it new life and application I have created an ecosystem for the material. I hope my work demonstrates the possibility of using new, but most importantly, pre-existing materials that need a second life, to create beautiful and thought-provoking work. Some of the greatest misconceptions of sustainability in design that I have had experience with normally come down to time and budget restrictions. Some brands come to me wanting to switch to more sustainable materials but expect things to be made quickly and with low budgets. This isn’t possible. Sustainability has to be introduced to projects in the early stages, it has to be well considered and given time and money and not just simply an after or last-minute thought to make a project or brand seem sustainable. It takes time and commitment to make these changes.

DC: Of other makers and sculptors in cities across the world working with industrial waste materials, whose work stands out as having had the greatest impact on you?

CK: Fernando Mastrangelo, Jeff Martin, Faye Toogood, Max Lamb, Nick Ross, Dirk Vander Kooij.

DC: For those interested, which design schools across the globe would you say offer a substantial material design program?

CK: Central Saint Martins Royal College of Art Eindoven These are just the ones I am familiar with - I am sure there are many more.

DC: What is coming up next for you?

CK: I am in the middle of creating a limited series of vessels for a design agency here in London as well as having multiple conversations with UK and international galleries about new work. I am also showing new self-initiated pieces at London Design Festival in September and London Craft Week in October.

‘ I would love for my work to be part of permanent collection in a museum. A museum would be the ultimate goal. ’ — CHARLOTTE KIDGER.
